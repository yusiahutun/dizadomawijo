Marilyn Monroe's first shoot as a pin-up girl aged 22 is featured alongside early calendar shots

Posted by Reinaldo Massengill on Saturday, March 16, 2024

Marilyn Monroe's first shoot as a pin-up girl, as she struggled to make it in Hollywood aged just 22, has been included in a new book's massive array of 40s, 50s and 60s glamour shots.

The image of the budding starlet sprawled out on a red velvet couch was taken as she struggled find fame in Tinseltown.

Just shy of her 23rd birthday, the actress was likely keen to earn some extra cash as she posed for her debut pin-up shoot in 1949 for photographer Tom Kelley.

Marilyn Monroe's first shoot as a pin-up girl as she struggled to make it in Hollywood aged just 22. Photographed by Tom Kelley in 1949

She made $50 from the image, which was later featured in the first ever Playboy issue in 1953.  

Compiled by internationally acclaimed and award-winning photographer and author Jon Ortner, the collection of over 350 photographs is one of the largest of its kind - now available in a book called Calendar Girls, Sex Goddesses and Pin-up Queens of the '40s, '50s and '60s.

In another photo, Diana Dors is pictured in a lacy black one-piece suit and her hair perfectly coiffed, seated on a pin sofa as she posed fiercely for the camera. 

Diana Dors was an English film actress from Wiltshire, known as 'the English Marilyn Monroe', due to their similar aesthetic.

She came to notoriety for her risque modelling and lavish headline-making parties, and featured as a leading role in films including Diamond City (1949) and Dance Hall (1950).

Diana Dors is pictured in a lacy black one-piece suit and her hair perfectly coiffed, seated on a lavish pin sofa

Jayne Mansfield wears a purple two piece set as she stretches out on a towel by the pool, with the incription 'Jayne' in the corner 

Mr Ortner has spent more than three decades assembling the photos from the era in celebration of the female figure.

The collection of over 350 photographs is one of the largest of its kind - now available in a book

Many images were never-before-seen in the photography world. 

Actress Jayne Mansfield also features in the shots. She was known a magnet for trashy gossip magazines, which she exploited to heighten her profile despite the outlandish rumours.

The original Hollywood 'blonde bombshell', Mansfield was hired by studios like Warner Bros and 20th Century Fox to be the next Marilyn Monroe.

She most famously starred in The Girl Can't Help It in her late 1950s heyday and later in Promises! Promises! as the fist major U.S. actress to appear nude.

The 34-year-old was a sought-after celebrity even as her film bankability waned in the 1960s and she sometimes had to sing in cheap nightclubs. 

Mansfield died in June 1967 after the car she was driving crashed into the rear of a trailer truck on U.S. Route 90 east of New Orleans, Louisiana.

The crash killed Mansfield and her boyfriend Sam Brody after her Satanic lover Anton LaVey said he had placed a hex on Brody.

The album contains pictures of the 20th century's most alluring models posing in sizzling shots

Here a woman is pictured holding up a telephone while smiling at the camera lens in a sheer cover-up

Various Hollywood Glamour Girls, Pin-up queens, 'The Girl Next Door', and international models are featured in the photos.

One picture shows a blonde model basking under the sun in a pool floatie wearing a strappy bikini, while another picture shows models posed kneeling down with fabric barely covering their modesty.

Some of the girls were completely nude, while others shield themselves with flowers, a wine glass and even an umbrella.  

At the time of the photograph, Marilyn had been released from contracts with both 20th Century Fox and Columbia Pictures, and turned back to modeling to make ends meet.

A woman frolics in a hay barn with daisies as she wears a red and white chiffon dress

That's how she was convinced by photographer Tom Kelley to pose nude. With Mr. Kelley's wife, Natalie, present to calm her nerves, Marilyn laid against a red velvet backdrop while nearly naked, staring sensuously into the camera. 

Marilyn signed the release as 'Mona Monroe' to protect herself - and also because she was embarrassed and ashamed.

In the book Marilyn: Her Life in Her Own Words, Marilyn herself explained that she only agreed to Mr. Kelley's request out of 'desperation', as she believed 'nice girls did not pose in the nude'.

Wrapped up! A woman presents herself as a present wrapped in plastic and a red bow in one alluring shot

Sheer fabrics make for risque shots in many of the photos which exhibit the fashions of the time

One woman is seen wearing peep toe shoes along with a black lacy bodysuit, gloves and a large brimmed hat

'You must promise to never tell anyone about my posing for you in the nude,' she reportedly told him. 'I want you to promise me that you will take the pictures so that I wouldn't be recognizable in them.'

Mr. Kelly sold the pictures to the Western Lithograph Company for $900, which printed a shot of Marilyn in its Golden Dreams calendar in 1951.

The printer even had to create additional versions of the picture, as at the time it was illegal to send a nude photo in the mail - which explains why there are nearly identical shots in which Marilyn is seen in a bra and a lace teddy.

The hardcover calendar book, published by Schiffer, is available to purchase on Amazon.
